Professional Communication

Greetings, small talk, telephone English, and building rapport in the workplace

Why This Matters

First impressions in business are formed within seconds. Whether meeting a client, joining a video call, or answering the phone, the language you use sets the tone for the entire relationship. Professional English is not just correct — it is warm, clear, and confident.

Formal vs Informal Greetings

SituationFormalInformal (colleagues)
First meeting"Pleased to meet you, Mr Chen.""Great to finally meet you!"
Morning"Good morning, Ms Karimi.""Morning! How are things?"
After absence"Welcome back. I trust your trip was productive.""Good to have you back! How was it?"

Small Talk — Safe Topics & What to Avoid

Safe: the weather, your commute, local area, general sport, weekend plans (vague), the venue or building.

Avoid: politics, religion, personal finances, health, relationship status — these create discomfort.

Alex
"Did you have far to travel today?"
Sara
"About an hour — the train was surprisingly on time, actually."
Alex
"That's always a bonus. Can I get you a coffee before we start?"

Telephone English — Key Phrases

FunctionPhrase
Answering"Good morning, NYLC, Sarah speaking. How can I help?"
Asking for someone"Could I speak to Mr Hartley, please?"
Leaving a message"Could you tell him I called? I'll try again this afternoon."
Asking to repeat"I'm sorry, could you repeat that? The line isn't very clear."
Ending the call"Thank you for your time. I look forward to speaking again."

Key Vocabulary

rapport
a warm, trusting relationship
"We quickly built a good rapport with the client."
agenda
a list of items to be discussed
"The agenda has been circulated in advance."
colleague
a person you work with
"I'll introduce you to my colleague from legal."
follow up
to take further action on something
"I'll follow up with an email this afternoon."
available
free and able to be reached
"Is the director available for a call tomorrow?"
brief (v.)
to give a summary of key information
"Can you brief me before the meeting starts?"

Business Emails & Correspondence

Writing clear, professional emails for every workplace situation

The Five Golden Rules

1
Subject line: Be specific. "Follow-up from Tuesday — action points" beats "Hi".
2
Salutation: "Dear Mr Chen," (formal) or "Hi Sarah," (colleague). Never "Dear Sir/Madam" if you know the name.
3
Opening line: State your purpose immediately. "I am writing to confirm our meeting on Thursday."
4
Body: One idea per paragraph. Short sentences. Use bullet points for lists of three or more.
5
Closing: Include a clear next step, then: "Kind regards," or "Best regards,"

Phrases by Function

FunctionPhrase
Opening"I am writing with regard to..." / "Further to our conversation..."
Requesting"Could you please send me..." / "I would be grateful if you could..."
Apologising"I apologise for the delay." / "I am sorry for any inconvenience caused."
Attaching"Please find attached..." / "I have attached the report for your review."
Declining"Unfortunately, I am unable to attend..." / "I regret to inform you that..."
Closing"I look forward to hearing from you." / "Please do not hesitate to contact me."

Formal vs Semi-formal Tone

Formal — external client

"Dear Ms Watkins,
I am writing to follow up on our meeting of 12th April and to confirm the revised delivery schedule. Please find the updated timeline attached for your approval.
Yours sincerely, David Marsh"

Semi-formal — internal colleague

"Hi Laura, Quick update on the delivery — I've attached the revised schedule. Can you check if the new dates work for you? Thanks, David"

Key Vocabulary

cc (carbon copy)
to copy another person on an email
attachment
a file sent alongside an email
pending
awaiting action or a decision
sign off
to formally approve or end communication
loop in
to include someone in a communication
action point
a specific task agreed for someone to complete

Meetings & Discussions

Chair meetings, contribute effectively, and handle disagreement diplomatically

Chairing a Meeting

StageUseful phrases
Opening"Let's get started. We have a lot to cover today."
Introducing agenda"Today we need to discuss three things: firstly... secondly... and finally..."
Inviting input"What's your view on this, Maria?" / "Does anyone have anything to add?"
Moving on"Right, let's move on to the next item."
Summarising"So, to summarise what we've agreed..."
Closing"That's everything. I'll circulate the minutes by end of day."

Agreeing, Disagreeing & Interrupting

Chair
"So the proposal is to increase the budget by 15%. Any thoughts?"
Tom
"I completely agree — the figures clearly support it."
Priya
"I take your point, but I'm not sure the timing is right. Could we revisit this next quarter?"
Tom
"Sorry to interrupt — if I could just add one thing — the deadline is fixed, so we can't delay."
Chair
"Fair point. Let's note both concerns and make a decision by end of week."

Key Meeting Phrases

FunctionPhrase
Agreeing"Absolutely." / "That's a good point." / "I'm in full agreement."
Partly agreeing"I see what you mean, but..." / "That's true to some extent, however..."
Disagreeing (polite)"I'm afraid I don't quite agree." / "I'd like to respectfully challenge that view."
Interrupting"Could I just come in here?" / "Sorry to jump in, but..."
Clarifying"What do you mean exactly by...?" / "Could you elaborate on that?"

Presentations & Public Speaking

Structure, deliver, and follow up on compelling business presentations

The Classic Presentation Structure

1
Opening (10%): Hook — a bold question, surprising statistic, or striking statement. State your objective.
2
Signpost (5%): "I'll begin by looking at... then move to... and finally..."
3
Main content (70%): 3–4 clear points. Evidence, examples, visuals. Logical connections.
4
Summary (10%): "To summarise, the three key takeaways are..."
5
Close + Q&A (5%): Memorable final line. "I'm happy to take any questions."

Signposting Language

FunctionPhrase
Starting"Good morning, everyone. Today I'd like to talk to you about..."
Moving forward"Moving on to my next point..." / "This brings me to..."
Referring to visuals"As you can see from this slide..." / "This graph illustrates..."
Emphasising"The key point here is..." / "What's particularly important is..."
Concluding"In conclusion,..." / "To sum up..." / "My final point is..."

Delivery Tips

  • Speak at 120–140 words per minute — comfortable and clear for audiences.
  • Use deliberate pauses after key points — silence is powerful, not awkward.
  • Eye contact — move your gaze across the room, not just to one person.
  • Vary tone and volume — a monotone voice loses the audience within two minutes.
  • Replace filler words ("um", "uh", "sort of") with a confident pause.

Key Vocabulary

signpost
language that guides the listener through the talk
hook
an opening that captures attention immediately
takeaway
the key message the audience should remember
Q&A
questions and answers session after a presentation
slide deck
a set of presentation slides
brief (n.)
a short summary or set of instructions

Reports & Formal Writing

Business letters, formal reports, memos, and proposals

The Business Letter — 5 Parts

1
Heading: Your address and the date.
2
Inside address: Recipient's full name, title, and address.
3
Salutation: "Dear Mr Hayashi," — always with a comma. Unknown: "Dear Sir or Madam,"
4
Body: Para 1: purpose. Para 2–3: main content. Para 4: closing action/request.
5
Signature: "Yours sincerely," (known name) or "Yours faithfully," (unknown). Leave space for signature + printed name.

Report Structure

SectionPurposeKey phrases
TitleIdentifies the document"Report on..." / "An Analysis of..."
Executive SummaryOverview for busy readers"This report examines... and recommends..."
IntroductionBackground and scope"The purpose of this report is to..."
FindingsWhat the research shows"The data indicates..." / "It was found that..."
ConclusionsWhat the findings mean"These results suggest that..."
RecommendationsProposed next steps"It is recommended that..." / "We propose..."

Formal Language Rules

Avoid contractions ("cannot" not "can't"). Use passive where appropriate ("The report was submitted"). Avoid slang. Use hedging for uncertainty ("It appears that..." / "The evidence suggests...").

Informal → Formal

"We got the data and it showed things were going wrong." →
"The data collected indicated that performance had declined below acceptable levels."

Key Vocabulary

executive summary
a brief overview at the start of a long document
findings
the results of research or investigation
recommendation
a suggested action based on findings
scope
the range of topics covered in a document
memorandum
an internal written communication within an organisation
proposal
a document suggesting a plan or course of action

Negotiation & Persuasion

Make proposals, handle objections, and reach professional agreements

The Five Stages of Negotiation

1
Prepare: Know your ideal outcome and your walk-away point before you begin.
2
Open: Establish the agenda and tone. "We're hoping to reach an agreement that works for both sides."
3
Propose: Make your offer clearly. "We'd like to propose a 10% discount in exchange for a 12-month contract."
4
Bargain: Conditional offers. "If you can commit to a larger order, we'd be willing to look at the price."
5
Close: Confirm the agreement. "So, just to confirm what we've agreed..." Then follow up in writing.

Negotiation in Practice

Buyer
"We were hoping for a price closer to €45,000."
Seller
"I understand your position. That's slightly below what we had in mind. Could we look at the payment terms?"
Buyer
"What did you have in mind?"
Seller
"If you're able to pay 50% upfront, we could bring it down to €47,500."
Buyer
"Let me check with my finance team and come back to you by tomorrow."

Key Phrases

FunctionPhrase
Making an offer"We'd like to propose..." / "How about if we...?"
Conditional offers"If you... then we could..." / "Provided that... we'd be willing to..."
Thinking time"That's an interesting point. Could I have some time to consider?"
Handling objections"I understand your concern. What if we...?"
Reaching agreement"So we're agreed on..." / "Let's confirm that in writing."
Walking away"I'm afraid we can't accept those terms."

Key Vocabulary

concession
something given up to reach agreement
bottom line
the minimum acceptable terms
leverage
advantage or power in a negotiation
win-win
an outcome beneficial to both parties
deadlock
a situation where no progress can be made
counterproposal
an alternative offer made in response

Business Listening & Reading

Strategies for comprehending audio, articles, and business documents

Listening Strategies

1
Before listening: Read the questions first — this focuses your attention on relevant information.
2
While listening: Note key words — numbers, names, facts. Don't panic if you miss something; focus on the next sentence.
3
Inference: Ask yourself — "What is the speaker's attitude? What does this word choice suggest?"
4
After listening: Review your notes against the questions. Eliminate wrong options using logic.
NPR Planet Money — Price Gouging

🎧 Unit 7 Audio
Listen carefully for: economic terms, speaker attitudes, factual claims, and implied meaning. You will answer comprehension questions after the lesson.

Reading Strategies

1
Skim (30s): Read title, first sentence of each paragraph, last paragraph. Get the overall argument.
2
Scan: Look for specific information — names, numbers, dates. Eyes move quickly, not word by word.
3
Read for detail: Go back to relevant paragraphs. Underline key facts.
4
Infer: What does the writer imply but not directly state? Notice word choice and tone.

Business Text Types

Text typeKey features to look for
News articleWho/what/when/where in first paragraph; quotes; balanced views
CEO speechCompany direction, values, numbers, employee messages, future plans
Business reportFindings, recommendations, formal register, structured sections
Press releasePersuasive language, positive framing, specific claims
Internal memoDirect tone, action points, dates, named recipients

Business Culture & Etiquette

Professional norms, cross-cultural communication, and workplace etiquette

Universal Professional Norms

  • Punctuality: Arrive on time or slightly early. If delayed: "I'm running about 10 minutes late — I apologise for the inconvenience."
  • Business cards: In many Asian cultures, receive and present with both hands. Read the card before putting it away. Never write on it.
  • Dress code: When in doubt, dress more formally than you expect — easier to dress down once you know the environment.
  • Digital etiquette: Mute when not speaking on calls. Frame yourself centrally on camera. Reply to emails within 24 hours on business days.

High-Context vs Low-Context Cultures

AreaHigh-context (e.g. Japan, China, Middle East)Low-context (e.g. USA, Germany, Netherlands)
CommunicationIndirect; meaning often impliedDirect; meaning stated explicitly
DecisionsConsensus-driven; may take longerOften made by one person quickly
RelationshipsMust be built before business beginsBusiness can begin without prior relationship
Saying "No""That might be difficult...""No, that won't work."

Sensitive Situations — Language to Use

Feedback
"The report is a strong start. One area I'd like us to develop further is the data analysis section."
Declining
"Thank you so much for the invitation. Unfortunately I have a prior commitment, but I'd love to be involved next time."
Mistake
"I owe you an apology — there was an error in the figures I sent. I've corrected it and it's on its way to you now."

hierarchy
a system of different levels of authority
diplomatic
tactful; careful not to cause offence
prior commitment
something already arranged that prevents attendance
cultural sensitivity
awareness and respect for cultural differences
